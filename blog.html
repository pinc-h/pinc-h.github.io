Uplands in bloom

May 2, 2024

I'm fresh off the plane, back on the island I've missed so dearly for 8 months. Yesterday was chaos: moving, seeing friends, salty air. It was the strangest feeling, like I was somewhere I shouldn't be. Back in Leith there's a humble bunkie with a muddy driveway and a dead silent lawn. Here, flowers are in bloom, far from the quiescence I was uncovering from the occasional snow. Yesterday, at the bar with my long-unseen friends, I revealed I'd never actually been to a Garry Oak meadow in bloom. Four years here (surely centuries ago), and I'd never made it out to see this endangered ecosystem everybody raves about. Hannah suggested I get a bus pass and go to Uplands Park tomorrow. So that's what I did.

Uplands Park is situated in a rich area of Victoria called Oak Bay. The streets are narrow and well-forested, expensive cars with learners-stickers plague the driveways. As I hop off the bus, blasting Steely Dan in my aching ears, I'm confronted with nannies finishing their morning shift. They're shuffling aboard the bus home. It is around 10am. I stroll the suburb for 10 minutes before finding the back entrance to the park. Last time I was here I attended an Insect BioBlitz, where a bunch of nerds much more knowledgable than me caught a dizzying array of bugs for iNaturalist clout. Now, I'm here alone with my decade old DSLR. I'm staring at every bug like it's brand-spanking-new, because they kind of are to my Ontario eyes. I see a lot I recognize, a few I don't. A few I thought I recognized I had the wrong name for all last summer. I was so hopeful the island had Tri-coloured Bumblebees (Bombus ternarius) last summer, but I've come to realize that they were all Orange-rumped (B. melanopygus). Oops. I saw two B. melanopygus, scurrying between low-hanging clover. I saw a very black looking bumblebee, which I really couldn't make out. Maybe a cuckoo. It would be interesting if I could find a B. impatiens, which are apparently shifting into BC. I saw bee-flies too, the really characteristic orange-fuzzy guys that hover much more efficiently than their Hymenopteran relatives. Bombyllidae I want to say. The sun was so bright and high all flyers cast a shadow, and you could almost tell the flies and bees apart by their movement patterns. I'm cracking my knees, bending as close to flowers as I can without a macro-lens attached. When I get up again (crack crack crack), the Camas has dusted my lens. I've been observing too hard. My camera is touching the world around me, and my camera is attached to me, and I'm attached to the world, is my jet-lagged logic chain. My cracking knees and ankles are kicking up nesting habitat, amongst thousands of flowers, amongst thousands of arthropods evolved to visit them. I put my camera away.

Clouds appear as the day lulls by. The park is still empty because it is a Thursday. I'm twisting my neck under the Garry Oaks, admiring drooping lichen and letting my eyes unfocus on it all. A cloud hides the sun and the light goes grayscale. Little glimpses of the St. Thomas eclipse I saw last month come back to me. That was a truly strange light, this is a weak imitation. But the lichen take on a new form, like the tiger losing it's conspicuousness in the eyes of a deer. When the sun comes back, a particular lichen I focused on starts to writhe. It's not lichen at all, but a yellowjacket on the underside of a thin bough. It's stripes were invisible to me in this mottled gray sea. A new way of looking at the world, thanks Annie Dillard.

Some notes for future mothing/what I've gleaned:
Without a proper net, moths should not be handled no matter what. The Copivaleria I handled moved erratically and was obviously injured by my attempt to get a close-up photo of its wing pattern. Despite the cold, I observed a decent amount of activity. There was little wind and it was a warm evening (for the area/time of year). The moths were definitely attracted to the light but failed to settle on the sheet for the first hour it was up. It is possible my movement/activity deterred them from landing. I suspect in the coming weeks a greater diversity of moths could be observed, with warmer weather projected. I will likely try again on a warm night, preferably before mosquito-season. The counts should be taken with caution as individuals came and left the apparatus frequently. For instance I observed the two geometers and two plumes hours apart.

Cisterns

I saw some cisterns on the Palatine hill. Due to the swampy geography of Rome, people have lived on this hill for a long time-- elevated above the muck. This is probably why the purported cave Romulus and Remus were raised in is on the Palatine slopes. At one point these cisterns sat at the entrance to Augustus' home-complex. It was in the modern excavation of these cisterns they found old coins, offerings to forgotten gods, and bones. These all sat beneath the Roman concrete that lined the bottom of the pool. Surely back then they knew these things were old... but did they care? Did the pits their ancestors were sacrificed into become garden ornaments?

Notes from the train into Rome

November 19, 2023

Some notes from the train into Rome, which happened a few days ago now but oh well.

A - I would like to learn some more about these Stone Pines. Their peculiar shape intrigued me, especially compared to the great evergreen Christmas trees my Canadian eyes are used to. They seem to represent the quasi-tropical climate on this latitude that continues to confuse me. (Side note, as of Nov. 21 I have seen 6 parakeets and 3 orange trees! Sorry to muddy the timeline, back to transcribing Nov 19 train notes). Okay to describe their shape: lacking lower limbs, spread out circularly at the top, all leaves (needles? Need a closer look) pointing up. The ecological sifnigicance of this, in my mind, could be two-fold. First, a cost-effective way to absorb sunlight. No costly lower limbs to maintain that acquire little to no sunlight. Second, abundant sun! Exploit this clime and specialize into sucking up every ray. However, a few problems I can't solve: why are they found in clumps, with no other plants taking advantage of the understory? Surely this limbless-trait of the stone pines was not adopted from competition with other trees, or to conserve space. I will look up more about this before I embarass myself further.

B - I had a bit of a religious moment on the train. From my plane the landscape was beautiful, but from the train, the Appenines took a godly form. I could understand Roman paganism from this vantage point. The sun cast through the clouds onto hamlets. Smoke rose from a chalet isolated from the town. Dozens of sheep, tended by a man with a stick... a Biblical scene. A radio tower on a hill that I was convinced was a steeple of a church, until we bounded closer. We reached 200km/hr and my ears popped going through the mountain tunnels. Terracotta roots in those towns, the same red as in the Pompeii I saw yesterday. A tractor, whose tires turned in slow motion to the speed of the train. This ferrovaria is a gift, to see the country with such ease. I want to take it to further and abandon my stop... an hour is too short to absorb it all.

Why, in all this space, had they decided to live so close together? All these little villages across Campagna. Some artifact of a deeper time, when we needed and leaned on each other, and couldn't get into our isolating macchina and go home. Each hamlet, with houses tall and crammed together on the hillside, looked like a family. I suppose they were.

I paid for my coffee this morning and the man said no when I got up to leave. He forced me to sit down and take my time. It was not a nicety. It was an insult to his service to move quickly.

I think if it rained here (in the country of Italy), I would cry. Surely this has never happened, as this is the last vestige of paradise on Earth. (Side note, it rained in Rome on Nov 20, I didn't cry but everyone's mood was definitely darkened).




Frankfurt flight

November 15, 2023

The CN Tower looks clean and crisp, must be the air this time of year. The summer-haze is gone. It's not even 4 and dusk has settled. The great tarmac serengetti is alive with roaming packs of luggage carts, and insectile fluorescent vests. The lady beside me doesn't speak English. This feeling is familiar, like a family holiday, but with no one to turn to speak to.

Coming into Frankfurt at night, the streets looked like suburbia. Surely this arterial American disease has not spread its way here. There were a lot more streetlights. Towers with way too many red airplane warning lights (I count exactly six on each rooftop). In between these curving streets were great pools of black, I associate them with lakes, but I've realized they're forests. The dimmest of tail-lights specked them, and headlights shone through these pits like flashlights in a horror movie, cascading cones that faded a few feet in front of them.

The windows fogged once we landed. The trees across the tarmac stood like slender white ghosts, barely perceptible in the black. The sun rose once I got to my gate. The clouds looked German: scrunchy and ready to say "Guten morgen" a bit too loudly. Everyone is busy and knows what to do. I feel like I might be the only one really here.




Another entirely too personal anecdote

August 4, 2023

My Dad and I both have ADHD. I remember his advice at 17: set priorities, don’t doddle, sit down and focus, really focus. I thought back then he wasn’t speaking to me, but to his teenage self. As I get older, I’m painfully aware of how much he was talking to me. To see one’s problems play out again in front of them is a horror my Dad never deserved. To try and earnestly prevent them again for me is more than I could ever ask.

On a related tangent (as he used to say), I’d sometimes realize 3 hours into our monthly 15-minute phone call that there wasn’t anybody on the planet like this man. And that these conversations mean more than the world to me. These phone calls would begin with “I have a midterm tomorrow morning…” Answered with: “That’s okay I’m halfway out the door to Home Depot.” 3 hours later, we’re talking about Cleopatra and the size of Roman carts. Or the glacial lake beds that everybody in Owen Sound drives over everyday. Conversations spilled out of us: it was the easiest thing in the world. It’s been the hardest thing in the world to lose.
Happy birthday Dad.




On blogs as a too-quickly forgotten medium and why I am writing one

March 7, 2023

I suppose I will start with a personal anecdote: I lost my father to cancer two weeks ago. Grief is a powerful motivator, and suddenly the fear of making a fool of myself on the internet has dissipated. Perhaps a stupid way to start this, because I will not be talking about it more. For my first blog post, let's talk about blogs.

Twenty or so years ago the internet changed how we share about ourselves. I was also born then, so I am partial to these first few years of blood-curdling modem screams. A plain-text HTML file became the modus operandi for the technologically literate, from sharing their day-to-day to weird little stick figure comics (I am particularly fond of xkcd thanks to my high school programming teacher). From what I have heard, as I was far too young to remember, people that started blogs began to have a bit of a reputation. Sharing all about yourself on the internet was not common in those days, before the advent of social media and its ilk. Blogging was not particularly popular: "geek" was still in the population's vocabulary and every negative stereotype associated with it. Towards the end of the aughts, as they are increasingly called, social media rose from the basements of Silicon Valley, poised to grasp our then well-functioning Aunts and Uncles into the tendrils of conspiracy groups a little over a decade later.

If my disenfranchisement with the state of the internet is not clear yet, I am disenfranchised with the state of the internet. There is too much to sift through and what is left in the sieve is not that remarkable anyway. In a time where this could all have been generated by a large language model, I find myself asking if there is any way to prove I even exist, let alone grab someone's attention. In an even bleaker aspect, how to grab someone's attention who is not used to reading letters on a lonely white void. More and more, I find myself on little islands in the world wide web, grasping to a forum here or a group chat there. The internet might work best in small communities after all, which I hope is what Mastodon has right. So, with humility, I am carving out this piece of the internet the old-fashioned way. Typing in an HTML file, committing to main, hoping what I write is entertaining or informative. This void provides no feedback of any kind to tell me I am doing okay. Slowly, I am learning not to look back so much and keep pedalling forward. Dad's got his hands off the bicycle now.
